Radium

Radium is a member of the Alkaline Earth Metals being the heaviest and only radioactive member of that family and like most member of its family is highly reactive. It pure form is a volatile silvery-white metal and when exposed to air will quickly turn black as a layer of radium nitride forms on it. It is a radioactive substance but it most stable isotope (Radium 226) has a relatively short half-life, at a mere 1600 years.

Facts

  • Was one of the first radioactive elements worked with by famous chemist Marie and Pierre Curie.
  • Due to radioactive decay it creates ionizing radiation and when combined with flurorescent chemicals can cause radioluminescence.
  • In the mid-1920s a lawsuit was filed against the United States Radium Corporation by the five dying "Radium Girl" dial painters, who contracted cancer as they licked their brushes to get a fine point.

Uses

  • Radium was formerly used in self-luminous paints for watches, nuclear panels, aircraft switches, clocks, and instrument dials.
  • Was used in products such as tonics and healing waters for its believed healing properties.
  • Currently its only use is in nuclear medicine for the treatment of cancers such as bone metastasis.
